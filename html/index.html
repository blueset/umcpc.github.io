What we do

This club aims to organise the University of Melbourne's competitive programming endeavours in order to create results that an institution of this calibre deserves. Simultaneously it is an attempt to create a tight-knit community of science-loving students who can experience together what college life has to offer.

The programming competitions that this club participates in are those that involve being given a set of difficult questions which must be solved through the submission of computer code. The problems vary in topic wildly, but the answer usually involves the use of mathematics or algorithms and is a program written in a language such as C++ or Java. While beginner problem sets may seem simple enough, prepare to spend days toiling away at a difficult question.

Above all other competitions, the International Collegiate Programming Contest (ICPC) will be the focus of this club's activities. The ICPC has three rounds, a divisional, regional and world championship. Divisionals have an open entry policy, but only the top teams will progress to regionals. Regionals is hosted across Australia and New Zealand, and luckily that landed us (Team O L D B O Y S) a trip to Auckland in 2015. Those who work hard enough can acquire a ticket overseas if they conquer the regional finals and enter the world championship. The typical ICPC round will require approximately twelve questions to be answered within five hours by teams of three. Don't expect to answer anywhere near twelve questions though. More information will be provided at meetings, and if you're unable to attend just message an admin over the facebook group or email us (details in the Contact Us just down the page).

Everyone is welcome to join. All you need is an insatiable thirst for knowledge!

About us

HUGE shoutout to the Monash competitive programming community for helping us get this far, we love you.

The conception of this club was what seemed like an afterthought Jonathan mumbled during the quiet flight over the Tasman Sea. We had just been overwhelmed at the regional ICPC competition in Auckland, our string of flukes slowly chewed through by five suffocating hours of failure. After welcoming the suggestion however, it became clear that it was far more than just a passing thought of his, my teammate was determined to establish a competitive programming club in our university. The first step was to enlist the aid of his comrades in coding.

Having shared in the lows of defeat and the highs of travel together we felt closer to each other than ever before. The sneaker-tearing climb across a dormant volcano, our fearful venture into a shady youth hostel (which was a wonderful place come sunrise), the oversized drinks at Wendy's, my futile attempts to find a fish and chips store, carefully avoiding cow pies in order to eat canned food at the summit of a remote country campsite while witnessing a breathtaking sunset, and of course sharing an intimately small tent for the night. All our experiences will forever be some of my dearest memories. During the elation brought by our companionship we eagerly accepted the proposition, dreaming also of success in the following years championship. Bearing an enormous ego, I joined with the condition of trading presidential status for team-captaincy, Jonathan preferring the latter. After landing however, the difficulties that lay ahead became clear and Daniel decided to part ways with the team. So here we are, naturally behind schedule, hoping that this story has a happy ending.

- Rami Ali

Meetings

26th March 5pm Old Engineering EDS5

Another simulated competition was held on uhunt. Scoreboard. See if you can finish off the problem set during the week.

Dividing Coins
 This can be done many ways using dynamic programming. The simplest is to keep track of the different amounts of coins that you can have in your hand by means of a boolean array, and then consider how each coin adds more possible amounts..

Wormholes
 This is a fairly straightforward application of the Bellman-Ford algorithm, solving for negative cycle detection.

Modex
 Use fast exponentiation (google it). You must also use modulo arithmetic - just mod after every single operation.

XYZZY
 A more difficult use of Bellman-Ford algorithm. If there is a positive cycle you have infinite energy - always winnable if it is connected to the destination. If not, you just need to check that the destination is reachable from the start, ignoring any vertices that have a best path energy of 0 or less.

Prime Distance
 This problem involves using a sieve of Eratosthenes. You use the seive to generate primes up to the square root of the upper bound. You then use these primes to seive the numbers between the lower and upper bound.

24th March 11am Alan Gilbert 101

Andy Lok gave a presention on the Bellman-Ford algorithm, which can solve the shortest path problem with negative edges, and also detect negative cycles. If you want, you can read his notes. We looked at two example questions.

Wormholes
This is a fairly straight foward application of the negative cycle detection in Bellman-Ford.

Hiking
This graph looks like you could use Bellman-Ford, and you could. However, you would never finish within the timelimit. We may look into algorithms that can solve this faster.

19th March 5pm Old Engineering EDS1

A simulation competition was held through uhunt, congratulations to the winners team Caviar! The theme of the questions was dynamic programming. Scoreboard.

TEX Quotes
 Simple string processing.

Maximum Sum
 Check the first ever meeting.

Divisible Group Sums
 A variation of the knapsack problem. Clever brute force using memorisation.

Humble Numbers
 The trick here is given in the sample output. You need humble numbers up to two billion, but there are only about six thousand of them. Generate, don't check.

Collecting Beepers
 This is a travelling salesman problem with 11 nodes. It's just barely possible to brute force with good code. If there were up to 16ish nodes, you'd need to use a variation of knapsack. The key is using bitmasks to represent different states of the graph.

17th March 11am Alan Gilbert 101

Jonathan talked about dynamic programming using examples such as generating fibonacci numbers and the 'Knapsack' problem. Dynamic programming is a paradigm that involves memorising certain answers which need to be calculated more than once. Google it to find out more.

12th March 5pm Old Engineering EDS1

The first ever training session! Students began to form teams and work on UVA problems together. The four problems from thursday were in the set, along with two low difficulty math problems.

Dermuba Triangle
 Back to some highschool trigonometry. Note that measurements are from the centre of each triangle.

Billiard Bounces
 Hope you've remembered your basic motion equations. Do this one mathematically, it's easier than it seems.

10th March 11am Alan Gilbert 101

In this meeting we discussed four problems. First everyone attempted to sort them by difficulty, after which they tried to find a solution on paper.

SMS Typing
 A few ways to do this one, hard code it if you have to.

Double Free Sets
 This was super popular, try writing it out to find patterns. It can be a process or a series formula.

Highest Paid Toll
 Bangladesh hype! You're going to need Dijkstra's for this one.

Barry the Biker
 You need to find a way to know which line Barry is on without calculating intersections or anything. After which you cannot use a traditional sort. Super hard conceptually, beautiful solution.
